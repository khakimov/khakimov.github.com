Arguments Markers

I am tall, and I am good at sport.

vs

I am tall, so I am good at sports.

When you use the word and, you’re simply stating the two facts. But when you use the word so (therefore, thus, hence, accordingly), you’re indicating that one of them is a reson for the other.

Kinds of argument markers:

Conclusion markers: so, therefore, thus, accordingly, they indicate that the sentece right after them is a conclusion.

After the words because, for, as, for the reason that, and the reason why are a reason or a premise. And we going to call it a reason marker, or a premise marker.

You can use Arguments in order to explain things. Explaining is gigivng a reason why something happened (or why it is true).

When you explain something, you and your audience both believe what you are explaining.

What are Arguments used for?

Persuading is making people believe or do something that they would not otherwise believe or do.

Justifying is showing someone a reason to believe the conclusion.

Justification tries to give good reasons. Persuasion tries to change your belief or actions (and bad reasons can do that).

When someone give you an argument, you need to ask a series of questions:

  1. Is the arguer trying to change someone’s mind? If so, the purpose is persuasion.

  2. Is the arguer trying to give some kind of reason to believe something? If so, the purpose is justification.
