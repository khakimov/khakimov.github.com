What is Enlightenment?

Rousseau wrote about being stirred (перемешивать) by his passions and he stirred passions of others. He is the conscience of the enlightenment, bad conscience of the enlightenment. He rejects it. He participates in the enlightenment and he turns against it.

One day I took the Mercure de France (newspaper), and galncing throught it as I walked, I came upon this question propounded by the Dijon Academy for the next year’s prize, ”Has the progress of sciences and arts done more to corrupt morals, or to improve them?” The moment I read this, I beheld another universe and became an other man. Confessions

Discourse - The Arts and Sciences

Have the arts and science contributed to morals? Rousseau expected people will talk about it in the way about improving and making the world better, as we’ve said that’s what enlightenment is. What could be bad? And Rousseau says quite the opposite:

While the Government and the Laws see to the safety and the well being of men assembled, the Sciences, Letters and Arts, less despotic and perhaps more powerful, spread garlands of flowers over the iron chains with which they are laden, throttle in them the sentiment of that original freedom for which they seemed born, make them love their slavery, and fashion them into what is called civilized peoples.

The arts and science cover up our oppressions. They make us forget that we’re not free, even worse, they make us love our slavery. They don’t promote knowledge and understanding, they promote tyranny and inequality.

The arts and sciences create new form of needs, new forms of dependence.

[Princes] know that all needs which a People imposes on itself are so many chains which it assumes… Indeed what yoke could be imposed on men who need nothing?

The arts and sciences become luxuries, they promote in us new forms of dependence. We don’t need them to begin with, but once we have the Arts and Science, then they are deprived of them at some point, we feel pain, we feel we’re missing something.

For Rousseua this was as creating new forms of dependences, vulnerability is a form of degradation and corruption, not progress.

What conclusion is to be drawn from this paradox so worthy of being born in our time; and what will become of virtue when one hat to get richt at all cost? The ancient political thinkers forever spoke of morals and of virtuel ours pseak only of commerce and money.

It’s important to distinguish two different ways in which justce can be tied to the good:

And this deprives (лишает) justice of its critical character.

  • non-relativist way of linking justice with conceptions of the good, principles of justice depend for their justification, not on the values that happen to prevail at any given moment, in a certain place, but instead on the moral worth or the instrinsic good of the ends right serve. 21. Justice, Community and Membership // 12. Death Personal Identity

Kant thinks that Aristotle made a mistake. It’s one thing, Kant says, to support a fair framework of rights within which people can pursue their own conceptions of the good life. It’s something else that runs the risk of coercion, to base law or principles of justice on any particular conception of the good life.

In order to investigate the ideal constitution, we have first to figure out the best way to live. Aristotle

Kant would reject that idea. He says that constitutions and laws and rights should not embody or affirm or promote any particular way of life. That’s at odds with freedom.

For Aristotle, the whole point of law, the purpose of the polis, is to shape character, to cultivate the virtue of citizens, to inculcate civic excellence, to make possible a good way of life.

For Kant on the other hand, the purpose of law, the point of a constitution, is not to inculcate or to promote virtue, it’s to set up a fair framework of rights within which citizens may be free to pursue their own conceptions of the good for themselves.

To be free person:

  • Aristotle: we’re free insofar as we have the capacity to realize our potential. And that leads us to the question of fit, fit between persons and the roles that are appropriate to them. To live up to my potential

  • Kant: notion of freedom as the capacity to act autonomously. Freedom means acting according to a law I give myself, freedom as autonomy. Consists in the conception of the person as a free and independent self, capable of choosing his or her own ends.

As free moral persons we are not bound by any ties of history, or of tradition or of inherited status, that we haven’t chosen for ourselves. And so we’re unbound by any moral ties prior to our choosing them and that means that we are free and independent sovereign selves. __We are the authors of the only obligations that constrain us.

Critics of Kant and Rawls - it misses something, it misses a whole dimension of moral life and even political life. It can’t make sense of our moral experience because it can’t account for certain moral and political obligations that we commonoly recognize and even prize.

Narrative conception of the self - man is … essentially a story-telling animal. That means I can only answer the question “what am I to do?” if I can answer the prior question of “what story or stories do I find myself a part?”

Once you accept this narrative aspect of moral reflection, you will notice that we can never seek for the good or exercise the virtues only as individuals… we all approach our own circumstatnces as bearers of a particular social identity. These constitute the given of my life, my omral starting point. This is, in part, what gives my life its moral particularity.

On the liberal conception, moral and political obligations arise in one of two ways. There are natural duties that we owe human beings as such, duties of respect for persons qua persons. Kant says that laws arise from a vertain kind of social contract. But this contract is of an exceptional nature. He points out that the contract that generates justice is what he calls an idea of reason.

A contract that generates principles of right is merely an idea of reason, but it has undoubted practical reality, because it can oblige (обязывать) every legislator to frame his laws in such a way that they could have been produced by the united will of the whole nation. Immanuel Kant

What is the moral force of a hypthetical contract, a contract that never happened?

John Rawls who worked out in his book a detail an acouunt of a hypothetical agreement as the basis for justice. Like Kant, Rawls was a critic of utilitarianism:

  1. Each person possesses an inviolability founded on justice that even the welfare of society as a whole cannot override… The rights secured by justice are not subject to political bargaining (переговоры) or to the calculus of social interests. John Rawls

  2. Principles of justice, properly undersood, can be derived from hypothetical social contract, not an actual one. (Veil (вуаль) of ignorance).

The way to arrive at the basic right that we must respect, the basic framework of rights and duties is to imagine that we were gathered together trying to choose the principles to govern our collective lives without knowing cerain important particular facts about ourselves. - That’s the idea of veil of ignorance

Veil of ignorance abstracts us from or brackets, hides from us, who in particular we are, our race, class, place in society, we’re healthy or unhealthy. Then we would agree to we come up to principles of justice. That’s how the hypothetical contract works

What is the moral force of this kind of hypothetical agreement? Is it stronger or weaker than actual social contract?

David Hume

When he was young, Hume wrote a book arguing against Locke’s idea of an original social contract. He said, “It was a philosophical fiction, one of the most mysterious and incomprehensible (непонятный) operations that can possibly be imagined”. And then when he was 62 he got an example of it:

house -> his friend rented it -> sub-lent it to third person and this person signed a contract with painter to paint the house and send the bill to Hume (he refused to pay). In court he said he didn’t sign any contract, that was true. Defance failed and he had to pay.

The main idea - actual contracts have their moral force in virtue of two distinguishable ideals, autonomy and reciprocity But in real life, every actual contract may fall short, may fail to realize the ideal that give contracts their moral force in the first place.

Feudal aristocracy. What’s wrong? Rawls says, the thing that’s obviosly wrong about it is that people’s life prospects are determined by the accident of birth, you can’t rise, it’s not your doing where you wind up or what opportunities you have.

Libertarian system. What’s wrong? Rawls says, it’s an improment because it doesn’t take as fixed the accident of birth. And even so, if you let everybody run the race, everybody can enter the race, but some people start at different starting points, that race isn’t going to be fair.

Merit based system, a meritrocratic system. Everyone regardless of their family background, has a genuinely fair opportunity. Everyone starts from the same starting line. Rawls thinks even that, doesn’t go far enough in remedying (устранение) or addressing the moral arbitrariness of the natural lottery. Because if your bring everyone at the same starting point and begin the race, who’s going to win the race? The fastest runner would win, but is it their doing that they happen to be blessed with the athletic prowess to run fast? So, even the principle of meritocracy, where you bring everyone to the same starting point, may eliminate the influence of social contingencies and upbringing, but it still permits the distribution of wealth and icome to be determined by the natural distribution of abilities and talents.

People may benefit from their good fortune, from their luck in the genetic lottery, but only on terms that work to the advantage of the least well off.

Example: Michael Jordan can make $31 million, but only under a system that taxes away a chunk of that to help those who lack the basketball skills that he’s blessed with.
