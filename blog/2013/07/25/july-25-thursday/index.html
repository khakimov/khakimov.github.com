Lecture 19: The good citizen

And the central idea of Aristotle’s theory of justice is that in reasoning about justice and right we have, unavoidably, to reason about the purpose, or the end, or the telos of social practice and institutions.

In general, it’s hard to do without teleology when we’re thinking about ethics, justice and moral argument.

The case of politics

how should political offices and honors, how should political rule be distributed?

When we discuss about politics and justice of this days, we mainly concern about the distribution of income, wealth and oportunity.

Artistotle took distributive justice to be mainly not about income and wealth, but about offices and honors. Who should have right to rule? Who should be a citizen? How should political authority be distributed?

Aristotle argues that to know how political authority should be distributed we have:

  • to inquire into the purpose, the point, the telos of politics. (Aristotle version about politics) Politics is about forming character, forming good character, it’s about cultivating the virtue of citizens. It’s about a good life.

It’s not mere life, it’s not economic exchange only, it’s not security only, it’s realizing the good life. That’s what politics is for, according to Aristotle. Instead, for Kant and Rawls politics isn’t about making us good, it’s to respect our freedom to choose, our goods, our values, our ends, consistent with a simular liberty for others.

Aristotle disagrees with them:

Any polis which is truly so called, and is not merely one in name, must devote itself to the end of encouraging goodness. Otherwise, political association sinks into a mere alliance.

Law becomes a mere covenant, a guarantor of man’s rights against one another, instead of being - as it should be - a way of life such as will make the members of a polis good and just.

A polis is not an association for residents on a common site, or for the sake of preventing mutual injustice and easing exchange. The end and purpose of a polis is the good life, and the institutions of social life are means to that end.

Those who contribute the most to an association of this character, should have a greater share in political rule and in the honors of the polis. The reason is, they are in a position to contribute most to what political community is essentially about.

That only by living in a polis and participating in politics do we fully realize our nature as human beings. Human beings are, by nature, meant to live in a polis. Why? It’s only in political life that we can actually exercise our distinctly human capacity for language to deliberate about right and wrong, the just and the unjust.

Political community is prior to the individual.

A man who is isolated, who is unable to share in the benefits of political association, or who has no need to share, because he’s already self-sufficient, such a person must be either a beast or a good. Aristotle

Living the life of citizen, ruling and being ruled in turn, sharing in rule, all of this is necessary to virtue. Aristotle degines happiness not as maximizing the balance of pleasure over pain, but as an acitivity, an activity of the soul in accordance with virtue.

Virtue is only something we can acquire by practicing, by exercising the virtues. It’s the kind of thing we can learn by doing, it’s doesn’t come from book learning.
